Len CwiklinskiThe PessimistRead

The White Sox are not a team. They’re a collection of misfits, overpaid deadweight, and decisions that make you want to throw your hot dog across the ballpark. Yesterday’s 0-2 loss to Kansas City wasn’t just another game—it was a mirror held up to a franchise that’s been staring at its own incompetence for a decade. You can feel it in the air, the way the fans groan when the scoreboard ticks forward, the way the players look at each other like they’re waiting for someone to explain why this is happening.

Reinsdorf’s stubbornness is the problem. Not the ownership, mind you—no, that’s too kind. It’s the refusal to acknowledge that the White Sox are a broken machine. The 2006 sale was a chance to start over, to rebuild with real talent instead of nostalgia. But no, Jerry had to cling to his ego, his tax deductions, and the illusion that the same old team could just… *fix itself*. Now we’re here, 5-9, 3.0 games back, and the front office is still arguing over whether to trade away the next prospect or let him rot in AAA.

The real tragedy isn’t the losses. It’s the way the team’s identity has been erased. Once, the White Sox were a powerhouse, a symbol of Chicago grit. Now they’re a cautionary tale—proof that you don’t need to be a genius to ruin a baseball team. The front office acts like they’re running a chess match, but they’re just playing checkers with a deck of bad cards.
